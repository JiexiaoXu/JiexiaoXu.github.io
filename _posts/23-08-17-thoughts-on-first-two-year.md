# Background

## Intro
Unlike other American universities, majority of students comes to University of Washington without a major. That is, with few exception of direct admit, most of us enrolled in the university with pre-major identity, such as pre-science, and will spend at least two quarters striving to get into the major.

Now, my sophomore study has almost came to an end and fortunately I got accepted by computer science program offered by the Paul G. Allen School, so I would like to share my experience. Hopefully, this would be helpful for the ones who are lost at first in our major system and ones who are interested to learn more about udub (short for University of Washington).

>This post is inspired by [AdroitAmateur](https://www.reddit.com/r/udub/comments/f9s27t/how_to_get_in_to_uw_cs_all_my_stats_and_essays/). Many thanks to his/her reddit for letting me realize that getting into cse major at udub is not impossible.

## Major System Explained 

There are three types of major at udub:

Open Major 
: can be declared at any time by any student in good academic standing.

Minimun Requirements Major
: have additional admission requirements (i.e you need to complete several courses to declare major, example: Art) 

Capacity Constrained Major
: require additional requirements and selective screening 

Open major and Minimum Requirement major are not necesarrily worse than Capacity Constrained majors. You can possibly find your passion at Oceanography or Language Studies. In other word, discovering a few fun courses and declaring major or minor relavant to that is totally a good way to enrich your experience at udub.

Notably, a couple of capacity constrained majors is notoriously tough to get into -- [CS](https://www.cs.washington.edu/academics/ugrad/admissions/currentuw) and [ACMS](https://acms.washington.edu/program-requirements-and-information), partially because they have fewer seats and tremendous prospective students. To get into your dream majors, you definitely need to spend more time and pay more effort at study than at your high school.

>It's a saddening **fact** that you need to outperform peers with same aspiration and persevere through a one-year-long or sometime two-year-long study. 
{: .prompt-warning }

>I recommend to have a **long-term plan** that includes the situation in which you are rejected. I was rejected for twice but my path didn't change a lot as I plan ahead. Plus, pairing with person with same goal and forming **study groups** would give you better chance for major application. It's always great to have a win-win condition.
