## **Motivation:**
Unlike other American universities, majority of students comes to University of Washington without a major. That is, with few exception of direct admit, most of us enrolled in university with pre-major identity, such as pre-science, and will spend at least two quarters striving to get into the major.

In summer of 2023, my sophomore study had almost came to an end, and fortunately, I got accepted by computer science program offered by the Paul G. Allen School, so I would like to share my experience through this post. Hopefully, this would be helpful for the freshman who are feeling lost in major application and ones who are interested to learn more about udub (short for University of Washington). 

>This post is inspired by [AdroitAmateur](https://www.reddit.com/r/udub/comments/f9s27t/how_to_get_in_to_uw_cs_all_my_stats_and_essays/). Many thanks to his/her reddit for letting me realize that getting into cse major at udub is not impossible.

## **Major System** 

There are three types of major at udub:

Open Major 
: can be declared at any time by any student in good academic standing.

Minimun Requirements Major
: have additional admission requirements (i.e you need to complete several courses to declare major, example: Art) 

Capacity Constrained Major
: require additional requirements and selective screening 

Open major and Minimum Requirement major are not necesarrily worse than Capacity Constrained majors. You can possibly find your passion at Oceanography (open major) or Language Studies (min req major). In other word, discovering a few fun courses and declaring relavant major or minor is totally a good way to enrich your experience at udub.

Notably, a couple of capacity constrained majors is notoriously tough to get into -- [CS](https://www.cs.washington.edu/academics/ugrad/admissions/currentuw) and [ACMS](https://acms.washington.edu/program-requirements-and-information), partially because they have fewer seats and tremendous prospective students. To get into your dream majors, you definitely need to spend more time and pay more effort at study than at your high school.

>It's a saddening **fact** that you need to outperform peers with same aspiration and persevere through a one-year-long or sometime two-year-long study. 
{: .prompt-warning }

>It's recommended to have a **long-term plan** that includes the situation in which you are rejected. I was rejected for twice but my academic path didn't change a lot as I plan ahead. Plus, pairing with person with same goal and forming **study groups** would give you better chance for major application, which is a win-win!
{: .prompt-tip }

### Background
Since I was only looking at the math-related major at first, so my delineation would primiarily focus on the science track -taking courses like MAHT 12x, Amath, and Stats. 

I graduated from a high school at Connecticut (I am an out-of-state student). It provides some basic AP courses such Calc AB and Physics I. Therefore, I could start from MATH 125 (Integral Calc). it also offers AP Computer Science that sparked my interest at computer science. Therefore, I did have a headstart when I come to udub. However, I would recommend people continue to take Calc BC and Physics Mechanic if their high schools provide, in that you could circumvent the tedious computation work in MATH 125. 

>Remember to check out the resources at [MIT_OCW](https://ocw.mit.edu/). Linear Algebra, taught by Professor Gilbert Strang, is a gem for those who wish to take a peek at college level mathematics (even it's still more about computations). 
{: .prompt-tip }

In comparison to other cse students at high school, I didn't heavily participate in competitions like AMC or AIME, obtain a software internship, or code some cool toy project at Github. That means, you can still be a member of the Allen School regardless that you later find your love in computer science. I initially set my target at Statistic program.  

### Freshman Year 2021 - 2022
#### Fall 
CSE 143 with Professor Stuart Reges GPA:3.8
  : Professor Reges designed CSE 14x series and have persisted to use the traditional grading scale instead of ESNU system. It requires students to hand-write the Leetcode easy problem during the exams while recieve assignments grade out of 20 points. Though I have learned most of Java basics beforehand, Professor Reges went over the entire basics in few lectures. His lecture quality is remarkable, as he could code while making concrete explanation during the lectures. One controversial side of his course is the style points. You have to check line by line to ensure that there is no Gotcha for TA to dock your points. At 143X, you can find quite a few peers in cse major. 

MATH 125 with Professor Alexandra Nichifor GPA:4.0
  : I had online course with Alexandra Nichifor. She explained things well, and her assignments are similar to exams, but Math 125 was designed as weed-out course that set heavy workload at daily homework. I was tired at the computations. It has same concepts as AP Calc AB, and introduced some extra integrating method like disc integration.

ENGL 131 with Instructor Kaelah Rose GPA:4.0
  : It's an usual expository writing course. Write one essay per week and submit your portfolio at the end of quarter. The grading as I heard is based on instructors.

**Remark**: This is my first quarter at udub. Major application was far away, so I chose to take class that is versatile for major requirements. Math, Applied Math, Stats, and Computer Science all demand great performance in MATH 12X series. As you spend time review the homeworks and past exams, you will be fine for exams. Somewhat I feel the entry level course become easier each year as I observed from the exam archive. I decide to take three courses and see if I can deal with the college level study. Students in CSE would probably take MATH 126 and CSE 143 this quarter, preparing them for CSE 311 and CSE 351.    

>Try to make the transition between high school and university smooth. Since we need to deal with roommates, new friends, and HFS, don't hesitate to drop a course if you feel any is drainning your energy. 
{: .prompt-tip }

#### Winter  
CSE 414 with Professor Ryan Maas GPA:4.0
  : This is similar with in-major course CSE 344. Professor Maas taught some basic SQL syntax and then introduce high level study of data base system including relational algebra and transactions. Without previous knowledge of computer system, I was lost in the hashing function and some deadlock conditions in transactions. However, the grading at exams is lenient, and homework is doable. The final homework is building a hospital scheduling system.     

MATH 126 with Professor Jonah Ostroff GPA:4.0
  : I took the online option of MATH 126 which focus on the vector calculus. Professor Ostroff went easy on the exams. Homework workload is less than MATH 125's. Therefore, I personally deem that MATH 126 is more of a breeze than MATH 125.   

AMATH 351 with Professor Lowell Thompson GPA:4.0
  : First course for me to learn about the Latex. Professor Thompson is very caring to students and responds to emails in timely manner. I was kind of scared at the rigorous grading scale in which you need 99% of overall score for 4.0, but Professor Thompson eventually curve everyone's grades up. The lectures delivers tremendous information but he will post the lecturs notes with all details. The exams reduced to the test of whether you memorized all the approach for solving ODEs. 

NUTR 200 with Professor Cristen Harris GPA:4.0
  : Nice course to have a deeper understanding of what we have eaten each day. Carbonhydrates are not evils, try less sugar, and finally happily eat! 

EDUC 401 with Richard Alejandro and Alison Horn GPA:CR
  : I learned about how to deliver diversity in the instruction. My teammates and I will form a teaching plan incorporating STEM knowldege and diversity education. During the spring break, We drove for four hours to tribal school and interact with kids. We endeavor to let students know that they can do STEM regardless of their identity.

**Remark**: Different advisor has different ideas to the upper level non-major CSE courses. Registering for CSE 414 or CSE 373 if you are curious about database system and data structure, since low grades will definitely hurt your chance for ACMS or CSE application. By the way, students normally need to take CSE 332 again once they get into major because CSE 373 didn't cover parallelism. Though there is waiver form, I haven't heard anyone who succeeded in waiving the credit for CSE 332. On the other hand, CSE 414 could count as the core credit in the major. Rather than MATH 207, AMATH 351 is usually a better course to take in that it require less computation and more methods. And you can get credit of 207 with 351. MATH 126 again proves to me that entry level course is easier during the COVID period as the exams no longer have the question that could kill 90% of students.

At this point, I needed to narrow down the scope of major choice. ACMS was an option for me with its DMA concentration. I aslo found out that computer graphics is particularly attractive. I applied to ACMS and then got rejected. I guess the main reason is my essay. I underestimated the importance of essay and payed little efforting at refining it.

#### Summer

MATH 394 & 395 with Instructor Harshil Desai and Jess Kunke GPA: 4.0
  : Math 394 and 394 each offers half-term course, which only lasts about five weeks. Two hour of math lecture was a chanllenge, and we had no TA section for review. This enables me to learn the material through reading textbooks. Overall, it's achieveable.


CSE 331 with Instructor Soham Pardeshi GPA: 3.8
  : Cyber version of Screwing bolt.


CSE 351 & 391 with Instructor Kyrie Dowling GPA: 3.9
  : Computer systems. You could prepare by utilizing the lecture video from CMU 15-213. This course allow partners to work on labs. The summer session has only 9 weeks, so she had to compress some of the class content. Overall, it's a great course for system programming.

**Remark**: Summer courses are often taught by the graduate students. The pro is that some graduate students know students better than professors do. The grading is often less harsh. Plus, some courses offered at summer don't have exams, which will greatly reduce the pressure. For the cons, you will see different cons from different instructor.

It's first time I applied to the computer science. I finished the essay a few days before the deadline but only asked one person for advice. The result turned out that I wasn't well-prepared. My essay still talked about the benefits of computer science, while the admission committee wanted to see something unique. That is, I need to put the focus on why I love computer science and how do I apply it to change something.

If I could go back to that summer, my path for my second year will not change a lot even if I get into the cse major because I would have to start with CSE 311. Three courses with heavy workload -- CSE 332, CSE 333, and MATH 335-- could have killed me in next winter quarter.   


### Sophomore Year 2022 - 2023

MATH 33X with Professor Stefan Steinerberg
  : My favorite one. Arguably it's one of the hardest series offered at University of Washington. We will re-look at the tools we learned at MATH 12X, making rigorous proof of differentiability and integrability. In the 336, we then worked on the Complex Analysis. Proofs are absolutely beautiful yet also painful for exams. Some tough homework dued purposefully at 10PM for the sake of "our good sleep". And he always hold office hour at 7AM, one hour before the finals. I learned a ton from Professor Steinerberg including his jokes and his passion to Mathematics. I can't believe how I understood and memorized all the proofs of half of the textbooks. And I could still remember the late night that I couldn't sleep due to excitement to the final and wrote proofs in the air. Though our passion wasn't reflected in our grades, it's a fantastic course for people who love math.   
 
JAPAN 10X with Professor Kaoru Ohta and Mazda
  : It's a great course for AH credit, it has less workload in comparison to other language course. I typically use my morning hours to memorize the kanji and new vocabs. The exams are almost same to homework. As long as you try to review the textbook before the exams, you will breeze through the entire series. 

CSE 333 with Professor Justin Hsia 
  : System programming course. My advice is try to do incremental work for projects rather than attempting debug after finishing every thing. First project made me stay overnight because of the bug on the heap. C++ is slightly different from Java. It lacks the garbage collection mechanism, but the syntax oevrall are similar for all programming language.  


CSE 373 with Professor Kevin Lin 
  : Data structure course with light workload. This would become the best course for tech interview as it go through the basic data structures and algos needed in the leetcode. Try start doing leetcode early as it prepares you for entring the giant tech company. However, I wouldn't recommend to take this course if you intend to apply for the Allen School. 

CSE 557 with Professor Gilbert Bernstein  
  : Computer graphics. Building nice rasterizer and ray tracer. The mesh editing has tons of works 

**Remark**: This year has more intense workload than my freshman year does. Nonetheless, I have been addicted on video games on my new laptop. If I could go back, I would spend more time on learning more about the graphics and systems. I realized that my passion lies in the graphics and HPC, which both demands long-term commitment. I then decided to start research with Professor Gilbert Berstein and joined his group meeting. I applied to the computer science program again, and fortunately I got in.

## **Conclusion**
Until the spring of 2023, I saw the rejection letter as the deinal of my effort. As I utilized the petition forms, saw peers in the major, and talked to Ph.D. student, I realized that a lot things we struggled does not matter at larger scale. You don't have to get full grade on every assignments. You don't have to spend immense time on extra credit of a course out of your interest. You don't have to take a lot of hard courses to prove you are smart. Only things matter is your sense of direction. If you want to do research, go read relavant papers. If you wish to work at Apple, Google, Meta, or Amazon, go do your project that fits your interest. There are so many fantastic open courses online, but few take advantage of them. The value of university for me now is not how rigorous their courses are, but is indicated by their people. Therefore, UW is great place to know people love CS including the students and professors.
